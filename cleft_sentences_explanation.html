Emphasis: Cleft Sentences

Cleft sentences split one idea into two clauses to place extra emphasis on a specific part of the message. The meaning stays the same — the focus shifts.

What is a cleft sentence?

A cleft sentence takes a simple statement and restructures it to highlight one element — a person, a thing, an action, or a reason. The word cleft means split: you divide the original idea into two parts, one of which carries the emphasis.

At B2 level, there are two main types: what-clefts and it-clefts. They look different and emphasise different elements — but both are natural in spoken and written English.

What-clefts

What / The thing + clause + is/was + emphasis

Use a what-cleft to emphasise what something is, or what someone does, wants, or feels. The emphasised element comes at the end — after is or was.

What / The thing + subject + verb + is / was + emphasis
Original I don't understand why I never seem to have any money.
What-cleft What I don't understand is why I never seem to have any money.
Original I like weekends because I can go shopping with my friends.
What-cleft The thing I like about weekends is that I can go shopping with my friends.
💡 What vs The thing I

Both What I… and The thing I… work the same way and are interchangeable in most contexts. The thing I… is slightly more emphatic and common in spoken English.

It-clefts

It + is/was + emphasis + that/who + rest of sentence

Use an it-cleft to highlight a specific element — often to contrast it with something else or to correct an assumption. The emphasised element comes right after it is/was.

It + is / isn't / was / wasn't + emphasis + that / who + rest of sentence
Original Friendship makes you happy, not money.
It-cleft It isn't money that makes you happy — it's friendship.
Original Maria called me, not John.
It-cleft It was Maria who called me, not John.
💡 that or who?

Use who when the emphasised element is a person. Use that for things, places, reasons, and actions.

  • It was Maria who called. — person
  • It was the noise that woke me up. — thing
  • It was in 1969 that humans first landed on the moon. — time/place

What-cleft vs it-cleft — what's the difference?

Both types add emphasis, but they work differently. The key is where the emphasis falls and what you want to highlight.

What-cleft It-cleft
Emphasises what something is or what someone does/wants/feels Emphasises a specific element — often to contrast or correct
Emphasis comes at the end Emphasis comes right after It is/was
What I need is a long holiday. It's a long holiday that I need.
More common for feelings, wants, problems More common for contrasting or correcting information
💡 Same sentence, different focus

Compare these two clefts from the same original — Stress causes most health problems.

  • What-cleft: What causes most health problems is stress. → focus on stress as the answer to "what causes it?"
  • It-cleft: It's stress that causes most health problems. → focus on stress as a contrast — "not something else, stress."

❌ Common errors

1. Adding an extra pronoun in what-clefts
❌ What I like it is spending time outdoors.
✅ What I like is spending time outdoors.
The what already functions as the object — don't add it.
2. Using that instead of who for people in it-clefts
❌ It was my teacher that inspired me the most. (not wrong, but less natural)
✅ It was my teacher who inspired me the most.
3. Wrong verb agreement in what-clefts
❌ What I need are more time and support.
✅ What I need is more time and support.
The verb after what… is usually is, even when what follows is plural.
4. Confusing it-cleft with a simple sentence
❌ It is stress causes most problems.
✅ It is stress that causes most problems.
Don't forget that (or who) after the emphasised element.

💡 Step-by-step: which cleft to use?

1
Identify what you want to emphasise.
Is it a feeling, want, or problem? → what-cleft
Is it a person, thing, time, or place — especially in contrast? → it-cleft
2
For a what-cleft: place the emphasis at the end.
What I can't stand is people who are always late.
3
For an it-cleft: place the emphasis right after It is/was.
It was the noise that kept me awake all night.
4
Choose who or that in it-clefts.
Person → who  |  Everything else → that
5
Check: does it sound more natural than the original?
If the emphasis feels forced, the original sentence may work better. Cleft sentences are tools — use them when they add something.
