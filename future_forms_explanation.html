Future Forms

English has several ways to talk about the future — and the choice between them depends not on time, but on meaning: your certainty, your intention, the evidence available, and whether you planned ahead.

will + infinitive

will Prediction (opinion) · Spontaneous decision · Offer · Promise

Use will when you make a prediction based on what you think or believe (not visible evidence), when you decide something at the moment of speaking, or when you make an offer or promise.

I think it 'll rain later — the sky looks a bit grey. (prediction based on opinion)

A: We've run out of coffee. B: I'll go and buy some. (spontaneous offer)

Don't worry — I won't tell anyone what you said. (promise)

might Possibility (weaker than will)

Use might when something is possible but uncertain. It signals less confidence than will.

I might go to the conference — I haven't decided yet.

She might not be in the office tomorrow.

be going to

going to Prior intention · Prediction based on visible evidence

Use be going to when you have already decided to do something before the moment of speaking, or when you can see clear evidence that something is about to happen.

I am going to apply for the job — I've already written my CV. (prior decision)

Look at those clouds — it is going to rain. (evidence visible now)

⚠ will vs going to

This is the most common confusion. The key question is: when was the decision made?

  • Decision made right now → will: "The phone's ringing." "I'll get it."
  • Decision made before this moment → going to: "I'm going to get a new phone — I decided last week."

Present progressive & simple present for the future

Present progressive Confirmed plans and arrangements

Use the present progressive for future plans that involve other people or external arrangements — something already set up and confirmed.

I am meeting Dr. López at two o'clock. (confirmed with her)

We are flying to Rome next Friday. (tickets booked)

Simple present Fixed schedules and timetables

Use the simple present for events that follow a fixed schedule — timetables, programmes, institutional calendars. The event is predetermined and outside your control.

The conference starts at 9 a.m. on Monday. (official programme)

The last train leaves at 11:45. (timetable)

⚠ going to vs present progressive

Both refer to things decided before now — but the present progressive implies an external arrangement or another person is involved. Going to is more about personal intention.

  • I'm going to study more this semester. — personal intention, no external arrangement
  • I'm having lunch with my supervisor on Thursday. — confirmed with another person

Future progressive, perfect, and perfect continuous

will be + -ing Future progressive — action in progress at a future moment

Use the future progressive for an action that will be in progress at a specific point in the future.

At this time tomorrow, I will be sitting on a plane to Tokyo.

Don't call at noon — she will be presenting her thesis.

will have + past participle Future perfect — action completed before a future point

Use the future perfect for an action that will be finished before a specific point in the future. Common with by, by the time, before.

By Friday, she will have submitted all her coursework.

By the time you arrive, we will have already eaten.

will have been + -ing Future perfect continuous — duration up to a future point

Use the future perfect continuous to emphasise how long an action will have been going on by a future point. Common with for + duration.

By next month, I will have been working here for ten years.

By the time the project ends, the team will have been developing the app for two years.

Form Focus Key signal
will be + -ing In progress at a future moment at this time tomorrow, at 3 p.m.
will have + pp Completed before a future point by Friday, by the time, before
will have been + -ing Duration up to a future point for + period, by next year

⚠ Future forms in time clauses

Critical rule — never use will in a time clause

After time conjunctions — when, as soon as, once, until, by the time, after, before — use a present or perfect tense, never will.

  • ❌ I'll call you when I will arrive.
  • ✅ I'll call you when I arrive.
  • ❌ As soon as she will finish, we can leave.
  • ✅ As soon as she finishes, we can leave.
  • ✅ By the time you get here, I'll have already left.
  • ✅ Once he has signed the contract, we can proceed. (present perfect for completed prior action)

❌ Common errors

1. Using will instead of going to for prior decisions
❌ I will apply for the scholarship — I've already started my statement.
✅ I am going to apply for the scholarship — I've already started my statement.
2. Using going to instead of present progressive for arrangements
❌ I am going to meet the director at 3 p.m. — she confirmed yesterday.
✅ I am meeting the director at 3 p.m. — she confirmed yesterday.
3. Confusing future progressive with future perfect
❌ By 10 p.m., he will be finishing the report. (implies in progress at that point)
✅ By 10 p.m., he will have finished the report. (completed before that point)
4. Using will in a subordinate time clause
❌ I'll send you the file as soon as I will find it.
✅ I'll send you the file as soon as I find it.

💡 Quick decision guide

1
Is it a fixed timetable or schedule?
Yes → simple present: The train leaves at 8.
2
Is it a confirmed arrangement with another person?
Yes → present progressive: I'm seeing the doctor at noon.
3
Is there a prior intention or visible evidence?
Yes → be going to: I'm going to quit. / It's going to snow.
4
Is it a spontaneous decision, offer, or opinion-based prediction?
Yes → will: I'll help you with that. / I think she'll pass.
5
Will the action be in progress at a specific future moment?
Yes → future progressive: I'll be travelling this time next week.
6
Will the action be completed before a future point?
Yes → future perfect: By then, I'll have finished the project.
7
Are you emphasising duration up to a future point?
Yes → future perfect continuous: By then, I'll have been studying here for three years.
