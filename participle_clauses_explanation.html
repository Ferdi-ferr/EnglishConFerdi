Participle Clauses

A participle clause replaces a relative or adverbial clause using a participle form — making sentences more concise and natural-sounding.

What is a participle clause?

A participle clause is a shortened version of a relative clause (who, which, that…) or an adverbial clause (because, after, while…). Instead of repeating the subject and a full verb, we use a participle — either -ing, -ed, or having + past participle.

Participle clauses are more common in written and formal English, but they also appear in spoken language. They let us combine two sentences into one elegant structure.

The three types

-ing participle — active meaning

Use -ing when the subject of the clause is doing the action — it has an active meaning. It can replace who/which + active verb, or while/because + subject + verb.

A The woman smiled at me.
B The woman was waiting at the bus stop.
combine → B becomes the clause
Result The woman waiting at the bus stop smiled at me.
-ed participle — passive meaning

Use -ed (or the past participle for irregular verbs) when the subject of the clause is receiving the action — it has a passive meaning. It replaces who/which + passive verb.

A The book became a bestseller.
B The book was written by a local author.
combine → B becomes the clause
Result The book written by a local author became a bestseller.
having + past participle — completed action

Use having + past participle when one action is completed before the main action begins. It replaces after + subject + had + past participle. The subject of both clauses must be the same.

A She finished the report.
B Then she sent it to her manager.
combine → A becomes the clause
Result Having finished the report, she sent it to her manager.

Defining vs non-defining

Just like relative clauses, participle clauses can be defining (essential information) or non-defining (extra information).

Defining

The clause tells us which one. Without it, the sentence loses its meaning. No commas.

The student sitting at the front asked a great question.

No commas — essential
Non-defining

The clause adds extra detail about something already identified. Commas required.

Maria, sitting next to the window, kept looking at her phone.

Commas — extra info

Where does the clause go?

The participle clause usually goes right after the noun it describes, but it can also appear at the beginning or end of a sentence.

After the noun (most common) The man arrested by police was released the next day.
Inserted in the middle — non-defining My neighbour, known for his loud parties, finally moved away.
At the beginning — having + past participle Having read the instructions, she assembled the furniture in under an hour.

Watch out for these

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Don't keep who / which. ✗ The girl who sitting… → ✓ The girl sitting…
  • -ing or -ed? Ask: is the noun doing the action (→ -ing) or receiving it (→ -ed)?
  • Same subject rule. With having + past participle, both clauses must share the same subject. ✗ Having finished dinner, the dishes were washed. (Who finished dinner? Not the dishes.)
  • Commas matter. No commas = the information is necessary. Commas = extra detail. Mixing them up changes the meaning.
💡 Quick test: -ing or -ed?

Replace the participle clause with a full relative clause. If the verb is active (e.g. who runs, which contains), use -ing. If it's passive (e.g. which was built, who was chosen), use -ed.

From relative clause to participle clause

One of the clearest ways to understand participle clauses is to start from a relative clause you already know — and simply remove two elements: the relative pronoun (who, which, that) and the auxiliary verb (is, was, are, were). What's left is your participle clause.

-ing: remove who/which + is/are/was/were
Relative clause Participle clause
The man who was working on the project is my best friend. The man working on the project is my best friend.
The students who are sitting at the back aren't paying attention. The students sitting at the back aren't paying attention.
-ed: remove who/which + was/were
Relative clause Participle clause
The letter that was written carefully was finally published. The letter written carefully was finally published.
The building which was designed by a local architect won an award. The building designed by a local architect won an award.
⚠ What about having + past participle?

This one does not come from a relative clause — it comes from an adverbial clause with after or when + past perfect.

After she had finished her degree, she moved abroad.
→ Having finished her degree, she moved abroad.

You cannot apply the "remove the pronoun + auxiliary" rule here. Think of having + past participle as a separate structure with its own rule: same subject, action completed before the main verb.

❌ Common errors

1. Confusing participle clauses with the passive voice

An -ed participle clause and a passive sentence can look almost identical — but they function very differently.

Passive voice Participle clause
The report was written by the team.
Main verb — this is the whole sentence. The report written by the team won an award.
Modifier — describes the report; main verb is won.
Key difference

In a passive sentence, was/were + past participle is the verb. In a participle clause, there is no was/were — the past participle works as a modifier, and the main verb comes later.

  • ❌ The report written by the team. — incomplete: where is the main verb?
  • ✅ The report written by the team won an award. — complete sentence.
2. Mixing up -ing and -ed

Ask yourself: is the noun doing the action or receiving it?

Receiving → -ed The window broken by the ball… (the window didn't break anything)
Doing → -ing The child breaking the window… (the child performed the action)
❌ The letter writing carefully was published.
✅ The letter written carefully was published. (the letter didn't write itself)
3. Keeping the relative pronoun

Once you convert to a participle clause, who, which, and that disappear entirely.

❌ The girl who sitting by the door is my cousin.
✅ The girl sitting by the door is my cousin.
4. Forgetting commas in non-defining clauses

Without commas, the clause identifies which one. With commas, it adds extra detail. Mixing them up changes the meaning.

Defining — no commas The students sitting at the front passed. (only those students)
Non-defining — commas My sister, sitting at the front, passed. (I only have one sister)
