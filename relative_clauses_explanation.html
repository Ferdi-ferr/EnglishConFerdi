Relative Clauses

A relative clause gives more information about a noun. Whether that information is essential or extra determines everything — the pronoun you use, and whether you need commas.

What is a relative clause?

A relative clause is a dependent clause that modifies a noun — it tells us more about a person, thing, or idea. It is introduced by a relative pronoun (who, which, that, whose) and comes immediately after the noun it describes.

At B2 level, the key distinction is between defining and non-defining relative clauses — they look similar but work very differently.

Defining vs non-defining

Defining — essential information

The clause tells us which one. Without it, the sentence is incomplete or ambiguous. No commas. You can use that or which for things, who for people.

A The book became a bestseller.
B The book was recommended by my professor.
combine → B identifies which book
Result The book that / which was recommended by my professor became a bestseller.
Non-defining — extra information

The clause adds extra detail about something already identified. Remove it and the sentence still makes sense. Commas required. Only which for things, who for people — never that.

A His first novel won the Booker Prize.
B His first novel was written in under a year.
combine → B adds extra detail
Result His first novel, which was written in under a year, won the Booker Prize.

Which, that, who — when to use each

Pronoun Defining Non-defining
that ✓ things & people ✗ never
which ✓ things only ✓ things only
who ✓ people only ✓ people only
whose ✓ possession ✓ possession
⚠ Key rule

That is only used in defining clauses. In non-defining clauses, always use which (for things) or who (for people) — never that.

When can you omit the pronoun?

In defining clauses only, you can sometimes leave out the relative pronoun — but only when it is the object of the clause, not the subject.

✓ Can omit — object

The pronoun is the object of the relative clause. Something else is the subject.

The film (that/which) we watched last night was brilliant.
→ we is the subject; the film is the object

✗ Cannot omit — subject

The pronoun is the subject of the relative clause. It cannot be removed.

The film that/which won the award was brilliant.
→ that/which is the subject — cannot omit

⚠ Never omit in non-defining

In non-defining clauses, the pronoun can never be omitted, even when it is the object.

  • ❌ His first novel, written in under a year, won the Booker Prize. — this is a participle clause, not a relative clause
  • ✅ His first novel, which was written in under a year, won the Booker Prize.

Commas — the clearest signal

Commas are not optional punctuation — they change the meaning of the sentence.

No commas → defining → identifies which one The students who passed the exam will move to the next level. Only the students who passed — not all students.
Commas → non-defining → adds extra detail The students, who passed the exam, will move to the next level. All students passed — the clause just adds information.

❌ Common errors

1. Using that in a non-defining clause
❌ My laptop, that I bought last year, has already broken down.
✅ My laptop, which I bought last year, has already broken down.
2. Missing commas in a non-defining clause
❌ Professor García who has published over 30 papers will give the keynote.
✅ Professor García, who has published over 30 papers, will give the keynote.
3. Adding commas to a defining clause
❌ The report, that was submitted late, was not accepted.
✅ The report that was submitted late was not accepted.
4. Omitting the pronoun in a non-defining clause
❌ The conference, Ø I attended last month, was very inspiring.
✅ The conference, which I attended last month, was very inspiring.
5. Using , which when the information is essential
❌ The drug, which was tested on patients, showed promising results. (implies only one drug was tested)
✅ The drug that / which was tested on patients showed promising results. (identifies which drug)

💡 Step-by-step: defining or non-defining?

1
Find the noun being described.
The scientist gave a moving speech. → noun: the scientist
2
Ask: is the noun already fully identified?
Is it a proper noun, a unique reference, or already established in context? → non-defining
Is it one of many, and we need to specify which one? → defining
3
Choose your pronoun.
Defining: that or which (things), who (people)
Non-defining: which (things), who (people) — never that
4
Add or remove commas accordingly.
Defining → no commas  |  Non-defining → commas before and after the clause
5
Check: can you remove the clause and still make sense?
Yes → non-defining ✅  |  No → defining ✅
